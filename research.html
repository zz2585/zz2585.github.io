Research



Working papers
  • Retail Investor Trading and Market Reactions to Earnings Announcements (with Friedman, H.)
We use trade-level data and exploit retail brokerage outages to examine how retail investors affect the pricing of public earnings information. Retail trading is associated with stronger price responses to earnings news during the earnings announcement (EA) window and greater post-earnings announcement drift (PEAD), but only the EA-window effect is robust to identification using plausibly exogenous outages. Prior to earnings announcements, retail activity is associated with less informative returns, but retail buy-sell imbalance during EAs is not associated with price responses to earnings news. We discuss several potential theories of retail trade in light of our evidence. Overall, results are consistent with a theory of retail traders as noise traders who provide liquidity that can help or hinder the impounding of public earnings news into price, but inferences are complicated because retail traders select to trade on earnings characterized by PEAD, potentially due to attention or overconfidence.

  • Financial Misconduct and Product Market Strategy (with Zhou, Y.)
This study examines the impact of financial misconduct announcements on product quality and product portfolio in the consumer market. Using Amazon consumer reviews and difference-in-differences analyses, we show that product quality significantly increases for fraud firms after the announcements, particularly when the media coverage is higher and the products are high-volume or more expensive. Additionally, we observe that fraud firms adjust their product portfolio by reducing the number of products they sell and replacing brands on Amazon following the announcements. We also provide evidence for two potential reasons for these product strategy adjustments: to regain consumer trust after the loss of credibility due to financial misconduct and to improve investment efficiency by eliminating low-quality production lines. Our study sheds light on how firms can restore their reputations after misconduct and the real economic impact of such behavior on product strategy and social welfare.
